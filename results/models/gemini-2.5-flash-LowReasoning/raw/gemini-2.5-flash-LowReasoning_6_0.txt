## Plan for Orbital Travelling Salesman Problem (OTSP) in C#

### Phase 1: Requirements Understanding (Completed)
The Orbital Travelling Salesman Problem (OTSP) is a highly complex variant of the classic Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP). Key characteristics include:
*   **Dynamic Targets:** Celestial bodies moving in their orbits.
*   **Delta-v ($\Delta v$) as Cost:** Minimizing fuel consumption, which is the change in velocity required for orbital maneuvers.
*   **Lambert's Problem:** Used for calculating orbital trajectories and $\Delta v$ between two points in space given a travel time.
*   **Time-Dependency:** Optimal paths are highly dependent on the timing of maneuvers and visits.
*   **Combinatorial & Continuous Optimization:** The problem involves optimizing both the order of visits (combinatorial) and the timing of each departure/arrival (continuous).

Implementing a full OTSP solution from scratch would require a robust orbital mechanics engine, a Lambert's Problem solver, and advanced combinatorial optimization techniques, which is a significant undertaking.

### Phase 2: Proposed Scope Definition and Research

Given the inherent complexity, I propose a phased approach, starting with a simplified scope. This would allow us to focus on the TSP optimization aspect while abstracting or simplifying the orbital mechanics calculations.

**Proposed Simplified Scope:**
1.  **Focus on TSP Optimization with Dynamic Costs:** The core task will be to find the optimal sequence of visiting a set of "targets" (celestial bodies).
2.  **Abstracted Delta-v Costs:** Instead of implementing a full orbital mechanics engine and Lambert's problem solver:
    *   We will assume that the $\Delta v$ costs between any two celestial bodies, for a given transfer time, can be obtained from a simplified model, a pre-calculated dataset, or a mock function.
    *   The "cost" function will take into account the current time, the starting body, and the destination body, returning a $\Delta v$ value and the travel time.
3.  **Time Propagation:** We will need a way to propagate the state (position, velocity) of celestial bodies forward in time. This could also be a simplified model (e.g., Keplerian orbits with simple approximations) or assumed as input.
4.  **Optimization Algorithm:** A suitable metaheuristic algorithm (e.g., Genetic Algorithm, Ant Colony Optimization, Simulated Annealing) will be chosen to solve the time-dependent TSP, as the edge costs are not static.

**Research Tasks:**
*   Identify existing C# libraries for TSP or optimization that can handle dynamic/time-dependent costs.
*   Briefly explore simplified orbital propagation models if a mock function is not sufficient.

### Phase 3: Design & Planning (High-Level, subject to scope agreement)

If the simplified scope is accepted, the design would involve:

1.  **Data Structures:**
    *   `CelestialBody` class: To hold orbital parameters (e.g., semi-major axis, eccentricity, inclination, true anomaly at a reference epoch).
    *   `Trajectory` or `Transfer` class: To represent a path between two celestial bodies, including start/end times, $\Delta v$ cost, and travel duration.
2.  **Core Logic Components:**
    *   `OrbitalPropagator`: A component responsible for calculating the position and velocity of celestial bodies at a given time (simplified or external).
    *   `DeltaVCalculator`: A component to calculate the $\Delta v$ and travel time between two bodies, given start time (simplified or external). This would conceptually encapsulate Lambert's Problem.
    *   `OTSP_Solver`: The main class implementing the chosen metaheuristic algorithm. It would iteratively propose routes, calculate their total $\Delta v$ (summing up individual transfer $\Delta v$s, accounting for time progression), and refine the solution.
3.  **Algorithm Selection (Example: Genetic Algorithm):**
    *   **Chromosome Representation:** A permutation of celestial body indices representing the visit order.
    *   **Fitness Function:** Calculates the total $\Delta v$ for a given route, propagating time and calculating costs for each leg.
    *   **Genetic Operators:** Crossover, mutation.
4.  **Verification:**
    *   Define a small, solvable test case with known or easily verifiable optimal routes.
    *   Test individual components (propagator, $\Delta v$ calculator if implemented).

**Next Step:** Await user confirmation on the proposed simplified scope before proceeding with detailed design and implementation.
