Here's a complete, compilable C# program that implements a scalable 3D bin-packing approach with axis-aligned boxes and rotation via dimension permutations. It uses a guillotine-cut style free-space management: the container starts as a single free cuboid, and each placed box splits a free cuboid into up to three new free cuboids. Boxes can be rotated (permuted w,h,d) to fit. The algorithm aims to maximize the number of boxes packed while staying within time/memory limits typical for the described environment.

Notes on behavior and choices
- Input: First line N W H D (number of boxes, container width, height, depth). Then N lines with w h d for each box.
- Output: First line packed_count. Then packed_count lines with: box_index x y z (0-based original index of the box and the corner coordinates where the box is placed).
- Rotation: For each box, all 6 permutations of its dimensions are considered to fit into a free space.
- Heuristic: For each box, we search among a subset of free spaces (up to a cap to remain fast on large inputs). We pick the placement that minimizes waste (free_space_volume - placed_box_volume). If a perfect fit (zero waste) is found, we place immediately. If no fit is found within the examined free spaces, the box is skipped.
- Free-space management: We deduplicate identical free spaces (same origin and size). We also keep a simple, robust approach without heavy merges to keep code compact and fast.
- Ordering: Boxes are processed in order of decreasing volume (larger boxes first) to improve packing quality.

Compilation
- This program is compatible with .NET 6/7+ (and should work with .NET 10 as described). It uses a straightforward FastScanner for fast I/O.

Explanation of key parts
- The code reads input, stores all boxes, and sorts them by volume (largest first) to prioritize packing big boxes.
- It maintains a dynamic list of free spaces (free cuboids) starting with the container.
- For each box, it tries all 6 permutations of its dimensions and checks against up to a cap of free spaces (MaxCandidates = 1000) to find a fit with the least waste. If a fit is found, the box is placed at the free space origin, the used free space is removed, and up to three new free spaces are added (the leftover regions to the right, in front, and above the placed box).
- Free spaces are deduplicated by a key (X|Y|Z|W|H|D) to avoid duplicates.
- The output lists the number of packed boxes and, for each packed box, the original box index and the coordinates of its lower-left-front corner.

Notes on scalability and performance
- The algorithm is a practical heuristic suitable for large-scale inputs because it avoids exploring an exponential search space and uses a cap on how many free spaces to inspect per box.
- It uses 64-bit integers for volume calculations to avoid overflow.
- It may not always achieve a perfect maximum on adversarial inputs, but it is designed to be fast, scalable, and produce a valid packing for large-scale scenarios (up to tens of thousands of boxes).
